Phase 2 · UNDERSTAND — the concepts behind the PM role

What does a PM actually do at a defense tech company?

At Anduril specifically: you own a product from concept to operator hands. That means writing the requirements, working with engineering on feasibility, representing user needs in every sprint, briefing leadership on tradeoffs, and being accountable for what ships. You are not a project manager (that's scheduling). You are the person who decides what gets built and defends why.

The defense-specific addition: you must also understand the procurement environment (ACAT levels, FAR/DFARS, OTA contracts), the classification environment (CUI, SAP, ITAR), and the operator environment. The last one is your advantage — most PMs here come from commercial tech and have never been the user.

What is Lattice OS and why does it matter?

Lattice is Anduril's platform play. Rather than selling individual hardware, Anduril wants Lattice to be the operating system for autonomous defense — the layer that ingests sensor data from drones, towers, radar, satellites, and human observers, fuses it into a common operational picture, and enables autonomous or human-authorized actions.

Think of it as: if AFDATC, GCCS-J, and a modern ML inference engine had a child designed for contested, comms-degraded environments. For an A-10 pilot, this is the system that should have been giving you better SA over the target area. For Anduril's PM: you need to understand the data flow (sensor → fuse → display → authorize → act) and where the gaps are in current operator experience.

How do you write a good Product Requirements Document (PRD)?

A PRD answers four questions: Who is the user? What problem are they having? Why does it matter (quantified if possible)? What does success look like (acceptance criteria)?

Structure: problem statement → user stories ("As a [user], I need [capability] so that [outcome]") → functional requirements → non-functional requirements (latency, reliability, security) → out-of-scope → success metrics.

Your edge: you can write the user story from memory. "As a CAS controller, I need the system to distinguish between a moving vehicle and a static one within 3 seconds so that I don't call in a strike on a broken-down civilian truck." That sentence — written by an actual A-10 pilot — is worth ten pages of user research from someone who's never been there.

What is an OTA contract and why does Anduril use them?

Other Transaction Authority — a contracting mechanism that allows the DoD to bypass FAR/DFARS (the normal federal acquisition rules) for research, prototyping, and production of innovative defense capabilities. Anduril has used OTAs to move faster than traditional prime contractors.

Why it matters for PM: OTA contracts are often milestone-based ("deliver working prototype by X date, demonstrate capability, then negotiate production"). As a PM you'll be scoping those milestones and making sure engineering can hit them. Know the phrase; be able to discuss it at a high level.

How does Anduril think about autonomous systems and human-in-the-loop?

Anduril's public position: humans authorize lethal action; the system enables faster and more accurate decisions. DoD Directive 3000.09 governs this — "appropriate levels of human judgment over the use of force." Anduril's products are designed for human-on-the-loop (system acts, human can override) or human-in-the-loop (system recommends, human authorizes).

As an A-10 pilot you have direct experience with ROE, positive ID, and the consequences of getting it wrong. This is your most important qualitative edge over a commercial tech PM: you understand the moral weight of the product you're building.

Phase 3 · COMMUNICATE — saying it out loud

These are the questions Anduril is most likely to ask. Work through each one out loud, alone, before the interview. Don't memorize a script — build a clear structure and let the words come naturally.

"Tell me about yourself."
The most important 90 seconds of the interview.

Structure: who you are → what you did that's relevant → why you want this specifically.

Draft: "I'm a USAFA grad — computer science — and I flew A-10s for [X] years. I went through Weapons School, which is the Air Force's advanced tactics and instructor pipeline — essentially a graduate program in how to design and evaluate complex airborne operations. I've spent the last [X] years [brief post-separation path]. I want to be at Anduril because I've been the end user of the problem you're solving. I know what it looks like when a system fails the operator at the worst possible moment, and I want to be on the side that makes sure it doesn't."

Note: don't explain Weapons School defensively. Name it, let it land, move on. Anyone doing their homework will know what it is.

"Why do you want to be a PM and not stay in an operator or analyst role?"

"The Weapons School taught me that the most leveraged thing a tactician can do is not fly better — it's write better TTPs, so that everyone who comes after you flies better. PM is the same idea applied to product: the highest-leverage position for someone with my background isn't being the operator who uses the tool, it's being the person who determines what tool gets built, so that thousands of operators are better off."

"Give me an example of a time you identified a user need and turned it into a solution."

STAR-L format: Situation → Task → Action → Result → Learning.

Frame: Think of any moment in the A-10, Weapons School, or your unit where you saw a procedural gap, a training deficiency, or a capability hole — and did something about it. Did you rewrite a TTP? Build a training scenario? Identify a mission planning tool limitation and propose a workaround? That is PM work.

Example structure: "During [deployment/exercise], I noticed that [specific user pain — e.g., pilots were spending 40 minutes pre-mission doing X manually when the system should have automated it]. I [action: wrote up the problem, built a workaround, coordinated with [relevant party]]. The result was [measured outcome]. What I learned was [insight about user needs / product design / cross-functional work]."

"How do you prioritize features when you have more requests than capacity?"

"I use a combination of impact and confidence. For Anduril's context: I'd start with operator lethality impact — does this feature increase the probability of mission success in a contested environment, or does it reduce operator cognitive load at a critical moment? Then I'd layer in technical confidence — can we ship this in a deployable state, or does it open a dependency chain that delays everything else? In the A-10 world we called this threat prioritization: not all threats are equal, not all threats are immediate. The discipline is the same."

Know the common frameworks: RICE (Reach, Impact, Confidence, Effort), MoSCoW (Must/Should/Could/Won't), and the simpler "what breaks if we don't do this?" test for defense contexts where the user consequence of failure is not a bad NPS score.

"How do you work with engineers who push back on your requirements?"

"I've spent my career in environments where the people I needed to execute for me could kill me with information I didn't have. You learn fast to listen before you decide. When an engineer pushes back, my first question is 'what do you know that I don't?' — sometimes they're right and the requirement needs to change. Sometimes the requirement is correct but the framing was wrong and I need to explain the operator context better. The debrief culture in aviation helps: we assume everyone in the room saw something useful, and the goal is to synthesize it, not to be right."

"What's a product at Anduril you'd want to work on, and why?"

Pick one and be specific. Don't say "I'm open to whatever." Suggested answer for an A-10 background:

"Lattice's CAS integration layer — specifically the capability that connects JTACs on the ground to autonomous sensors in the air. That interface has been broken for decades: the data is there, the connectivity has gotten better, but the operator experience hasn't kept up. I've been on both sides of that radio call — I know what the JTAC needs and I know what the pilot needs. That's the problem I'd want to close."

Alternative: Roadrunner / counter-UAS if you have experience with the drone threat. Ghost-X if you want to focus on ISR product management.

"Tell me about a time you failed."

Be specific. Be honest. Show learning. The debrief culture gives you a natural structure — pilots talk about failures every day, that's what the debrief is for. Use that frame:

"In [context], I made a decision to [action]. The result was [specific negative outcome]. In the debrief I identified that I had [root cause: anchoring bias / incomplete information / mis-prioritized / failed to delegate]. What I changed was [specific behavioral change]. The next time [similar situation] came up, I [applied the learning]."

Don't pick a story where the failure was small or where you're actually secretly the hero. Pick a real one. Anduril is a culture that values honesty over polish.

"How do you communicate technical tradeoffs to non-technical stakeholders?"

"I use the cost-capability-schedule triangle and I anchor it in the operator consequence. 'If we add this capability, we delay by three weeks and the system ships after the exercise window. If we cut it, the operator loses [specific thing]. Here's what I recommend and why.' The non-technical stakeholder doesn't need to understand the code — they need to understand the tradeoff in terms they can defend to their chain. I brief it that way."

"Where do you see yourself in five years?"

Don't be vague. "I want to own a major product line — something that's in the hands of real operators in real operations. I'm interested in the intersection of autonomy and human judgment, and I think that problem is going to get harder before it gets easier as the threat environment evolves. In five years I want to be the person who's shaped how Anduril has approached that — with a track record of products that actually work in the field."

Essay questions — write or speak your answer

1. Anduril is building a new capability to help A-10 pilots (before retirement) and their eventual replacements coordinate CAS with ground forces in a GPS-degraded environment. You are the PM. What's your first move?

Think: user research → problem definition → MVP scope → success metric. Reference your own experience as the user.

2. Engineering tells you that the feature the JTACs most want (real-time blue force tracking on Lattice, visible on the pilot's display) will delay the release by 6 weeks. The PM director wants to ship on time. What do you do?

Think about: what information you'd gather, what the operator consequence of each option is, how you'd frame the recommendation, and what you'd do if overruled.

3. You've been given a new product area: Roadrunner's human-machine interface for the launch operator. You have no existing requirements, one engineer, and a demo in 90 days. Walk me through how you approach this.

Think: what you'd do in week 1 (user interviews, existing pain points), what an MVP HMI looks like, what success at 90 days means vs. what a production-ready system looks like.

Phase 5 · ASSESS — quick scenarios

Scenario A: You're in a user interview with a JTAC who says "I hate the current interface." How do you respond?

Don't jump to solutions. Ask: "What specifically are you doing when it breaks down?" → "How often does this happen?" → "What do you do instead right now?" → "What would a good day look like?" You are mining for the specific behavior, not the vague sentiment. "I hate the interface" is data; it's not requirements. Your job is to convert it.

Scenario B: An engineer on your team says a requirement you've written is technically impossible. What do you do?

First: believe them and get specific. "What exactly makes it impossible — is it compute, latency, data availability, or time?" Then: understand if it's impossible given current architecture or impossible ever. Then: go back to the operator need. Can a different implementation satisfy the same underlying need? Write the requirement to the capability ("the system shall enable the operator to positively identify a moving target within 5 seconds"), not the implementation ("the system shall use ML fusion layer X"). If it's genuinely impossible, escalate honestly with a proposed alternative.

Scenario C: Your product ships to a unit and they're not using it. What's your first hypothesis and what do you do?

Three most common failure modes: (1) training gap — they don't know how to use it, (2) fit gap — it solves a problem they don't actually have, (3) trust gap — they tried it once, it failed, now they default to old behavior. Go visit the unit. Watch them work. Don't send a survey. Observe the specific moment they switch away from your product to the old tool — that moment is your requirements document.

Scenario D: Palmer Luckey asks you in a hallway "what should we build next?" You have 60 seconds.

Have a real answer. Something specific, grounded in your operator experience. Example: "The biggest gap I saw in the A-10 is the interface between the kill chain decision and the comms-degraded environment. Everyone assumes the pilot has situational awareness — but the moment the link drops, you're flying on last-known positions. I'd build a Lattice capability that predicts threat movement in the 30-second comms window — not to replace judgment, but to sharpen the estimate the pilot already has to make." Confident, specific, operator-grounded, connected to Lattice.

Your questions for the interviewer — ask at least three

Good questions signal you've done the work. These are calibrated for an Anduril PM interview.

  1. "How much time does a PM on your team spend embedded with operators versus in the office? What does that rhythm look like?"
  2. "What's the hardest part of the operator-to-product translation problem that you haven't solved yet?"
  3. "When a PM and an engineer disagree on a requirement, how does Anduril resolve it? Who has final say?"
  4. "What does a strong first 90 days look like for a new PM here?"
  5. "How does the Weapons School training — the TTP-writing, the debrief culture — translate to what you actually need in this role?"
  6. "What's the one thing you wish every PM candidate understood about Anduril before they showed up?"

Post-interview debrief — do this within 30 minutes of hanging up

Treat it like a mission debrief. Write your answers while they're fresh.

1. What question did they ask that you weren't ready for? What's the better answer?

2. What answer landed well? What made it work?

3. What did you learn about the role or the team that you didn't know before?

4. What's your honest read on fit — theirs for you, yours for them?

5. What do you do in the next 24 hours?

Amazon — Product Manager Interview

Same operator. Wildly different shape. Where Anduril hires you because you were the customer, Amazon hires you because you can become one.

Role
Product Manager – Technical (PM-T) — Amazon (could be AWS, Devices, Logistics, Prime Video, Alexa, etc.)
Your profile
USAFA '14 · B.Sc. Computer Science · A-10 Pilot · Weapons School graduate · Track & Field athlete · Separating 2026
The read
Amazon does not care that you flew the A-10. Amazon cares that you can deliver results, take ownership, dive deep, and hire and develop the best. Your job is to translate every operational story into evidence of one of the Leadership Principles. The military-to-PM-T leap is real but learnable.
Format
4–6 rounds. Behavioral STAR + Working Backwards exercise + writing sample + a "Bar Raiser" loop. Heavy use of LP-keyed behavioral questions.
The frame: Anduril is "you've been the customer." Amazon is "you have never been the customer, and that's fine — you ship faster than people who think they have." Your edge is operating tempo: you have run real missions on incomplete data and hit results. That's Bias for Action, Deliver Results, and Earn Trust. Translate every story to one LP per answer.

The Leadership Principles you should over-prepare

Amazon has 16 LPs. Three of them are where vets win and three are where vets get rejected. Pre-write a STAR-L story for each.

Where vets win

  • Deliver Results — sortie executed under pressure, mission accomplished with degraded resources
  • Bias for Action — no perfect data, decision in 30 seconds, lives on the line
  • Ownership — flight lead who carried the package, not the manager who delegated it

Where vets get rejected

  • Customer Obsession — "we" wasn't a customer in the military. Pre-write a story where you sought out a user need without being asked.
  • Dive Deep — "I had a sergeant handle that" is a fail. Find a story where you read the data yourself and changed the call.
  • Disagree and Commit — military says "execute." Amazon wants the story where you pushed back, were overruled, and then ran hard with the decision anyway.

The story bank — 6 STAR-L stories you must walk in with

Each story should be 90 seconds, tagged to 1–2 LPs, and end with a one-line Learning. Write them out before you walk into a room.

1. Customer Obsession — A time you sought out a user's actual problem (not the one you were briefed on)
Hint: a JTAC asking for a different effect than the ATO directed; a maintainer flagging a usability issue you took back to acquisitions; an Airman whose career problem you fought for.
2. Deliver Results — A measurable outcome under impossible constraints
Hint: combat sortie metrics, training pipeline you built, exercise you ran, BD/grant numbers if you've worked civilian.
3. Bias for Action — A decision made under high uncertainty
Hint: any tactical engagement decision; an investment call with incomplete intel.
4. Dive Deep — A time you read the data yourself instead of trusting a summary
Hint: stan-eval data; a maintenance trend line you caught; a hiring metric you re-pulled.
5. Disagree and Commit — A time you pushed back, lost, and executed anyway
This is the LP most vets fumble. Don't pick a story where you eventually got your way. Pick one where you didn't.
6. Hire and Develop the Best — A person you grew, named, with a real growth arc
A wingman you turned into a flight lead; a 2Lt who became a credible IP; an Airman who got into OCS because of work you did.

The Working Backwards exercise

Amazon often gives PM candidates a Working Backwards prompt. You have 30–60 minutes to draft a 1-page PRFAQ (Press Release + FAQ) for a hypothetical product. The press release goes first — written as if the product already launched.

PR FAQ minimum sections Headline (one line, customer-facing). Subhead. Customer problem. Solution. Customer quote. CEO quote. How to get started. Top 3 customer FAQs. Top 3 internal FAQs (privacy, scaling, legal). 1 page max.
Practice prompt: Design a product for transitioning military officers to find civilian jobs in defense tech.

Notice the trap: you'd want to build the most complete product. Don't. Pick one user, one need, one MVP. Customer obsession is "deeply understand this user," not "serve everyone." Cut your scope. Write the press release for that minimum. The FAQs are where you show range.

The 24 hours before

Build Your Own · Any Role · 30 minutes

Build a custom interview workbook with Claude

The Anduril and Amazon workbooks above were built using the protocol on this page. You can build one for any role you're interviewing for in about 30 minutes. Here's how.

The protocol: Open claude.ai. Paste in (1) your background, (2) the job description, (3) the company's public materials, (4) the master prompt below. Iterate. Print. Practice.

Step 1 — Gather your inputs

Before you open Claude, collect these into a single document:

  1. Your background brief — 200–400 words. Rank, AFSC/MOS, key assignments, schools, education, separation date, target geography. The version you'd give a brand-new mentor in five minutes.
  2. The job description — paste the full thing. Don't paraphrase.
  3. Company artifacts — at least three. Examples: their About page, a recent press release, the founder's most recent podcast appearance, the product page for the team you're interviewing with, their public values or principles document. Three sources beats one.
  4. Two real stories from your career — one operational success, one failure you learned from. These anchor every behavioral answer Claude generates.

Step 2 — The master prompt

Paste this into Claude after pasting your inputs. Edit the role/company name to match yours.

You are an interview prep coach for a USAFA grad transitioning out of active duty. I have an interview for [ROLE] at [COMPANY]. I've pasted my background, the JD, and three company artifacts above. Build me an interview workbook with these sections: 1. ORIENTATION — a one-paragraph read on why this company would hire someone with my profile, and where the translation gap is. 2. THE COMPANY IN 90 SECONDS — what they do, what their stated values or principles are, and how their hiring loop tends to evaluate. 3. THE TRANSLATION TABLE — a two-column table mapping 5 of my military experiences to specific competencies the JD calls out. 4. STORY BANK — six STAR-L behavioral stories I should pre-write. For each, give me the prompt, which competency it covers, and a two-line hint pulled from my actual background. 5. THE TECHNICAL/EXERCISE PORTION — what's likely (working backwards, system design, case study, product critique, etc.), with a practice prompt I can attempt. 6. QUESTIONS TO ASK THEM — five sharp questions specific to this team / product / company. 7. THE 24 HOURS BEFORE — a checklist. 8. RED FLAGS in my background and how to handle each if it comes up. Be specific. Don't give me generic interview advice. Use the JD and company artifacts to ground every recommendation. Keep it to about 1500 words.

Step 3 — Iterate

Claude's first draft will be 80% there. The remaining 20% is the conversation. Three follow-ups worth running:

  • "Re-write the story bank assuming I have never been a [X]." If you didn't fly, didn't deploy, didn't lead a squadron, say so. Claude will rebuild around what you actually did.
  • "What are the three weakest stories in the bank? Replace them." The first draft is often padded. This question forces sharpness.
  • "Now play the interviewer. Ask me five behavioral questions, one at a time, and grade my STAR-L answers against the LPs/competencies." Free practice partner. Don't skip this step.

Step 4 — Save and reuse

Once you've got a workbook you like, save the conversation. The next role you interview for, paste in the new JD and ask Claude to produce a new workbook in the same format. The protocol amortizes — your second workbook takes 10 minutes, not 30.

A real warning Claude is an excellent prep partner. It is a poor substitute for actually thinking about your stories. Type the STAR-L answers yourself. Speak them out loud. Time them. Get a human friend or coach to listen. AI gets you 80% of the way; you have to walk the last 20%.

What this protocol is NOT

  • Not a script. If you read AI-generated answers in the room, the interviewer will catch it. Use Claude to find the shape of your stories — not to write them.
  • Not a substitute for reps. The point is to pre-load high-quality material so you spend the actual interview being present, not constructing.
  • Not a guarantee. A great workbook still loses to bad sleep, a panic attack, or a bad culture fit. The workbook reduces variance — it doesn't eliminate it.
